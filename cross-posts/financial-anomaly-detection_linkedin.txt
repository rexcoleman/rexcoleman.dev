I'm a CFA charterholder who built an ML fraud detector. XGBoost hit 0.987 AUC. But the most interesting finding was what the models miss.

CFA-informed rule-based scoring achieves 0.898 AUC on its own.

Key findings:

- Domain expertise in rules captures ~91% of what ML captures. The last 9% comes from non-linear interactions
- 8 of top 20 SHAP features are CFA-informed (amount-to-median ratio, merchant risk tiers, suspicious timing)
- System-only features retain 81% detection even if the fraudster controls everything they can manipulate
- XGBoost peaks at max_depth=2 and degrades with deeper trees — simplicity beats capacity
- This is the 5th domain where controllability analysis produces actionable security insights

Rules aren't dead. The optimal system uses both: rules as the fast path, ML for edge cases.
